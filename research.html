Research

Langmuir turbulence

Ocean surface waves affect the air-sea fluxes of heat, momentum and gases, thereby the regional and global climate. One important wave-related physical process is Langmuir turbulence, which results from the interaction between the ocean surface waves and the Eulerian currents and turbulence. It strongly affects the vertical mixing in the ocean surface boundary layer (OSBL). I study the physics of Langmuir turbulence using large eddy simulations (LES) of the wave phase averaged equations. The above figure is a snapshot of the vertical velocity in an LES of Langmuir turbulence. Note the streaks of downwelling regions (blue), which are associated with Langmuir circulation with pairs of counter-rotating vortices aligned with the wind and waves. See more in my publications [6] and [11].

Diagnosing the turbulence structure

Turbulent flows driven by different forces have distinctive structures and anisotropy. I develop diagnostics to describe these distinctive features of various turbulent flows. These diagnostics describe the complex structure of turbulent flows in a compact way and provide useful insights into the physics of turbulent flows, including their evolution and interactions with background fluid properties such as the stratification. One example is given by the anisotropic barycentric map and directional map shown above, which help explain the effect of Langmuir turbulence on the entrainment at the base of the ocean mixed layer. See more in my publication [11]. MATLAB tools to generate such plots can be found on Github.

Modeling the OSBL turbulence in climate models

Small scale physical processes such as ocean surface boundary layer (OSBL) turbulence are not resolved in climate models, at least not until the end of this century (see Figure 1 of this paper for how this was estimated). However, their effects on the global climate are nontrivial. I develop parameterizations to represent the effects of such small scale physical processes in climate models. The above figure shows a comparison between 11 OSBL turbulence parameterizations with and without Langmuir turbulence. See more in my publication [9]. Many of these parameterizations have been implemented in the Community Ocean Vertical Mixing Project (CVMix) and the General Ocean Turbulence Model (GOTM), which are libraries that can be easily used in many ocean models.

Impact of ocean surface waves on global climate

A major part of my PhD work focused on assessing the impact of ocean surface waves on global climate, in particular the effect of Langmuir turbulence. I coupled a global climate model (NCAR's CESM) with an ocean surface wave model (NOAA's WAVEWATCH III) for this purpose. I also developed a computationally efficient statistical wave model for Langmuir turbulence parameterizations to avoid the significant computational cost of WAVEWATCH III. See more in my publications [4] and [5]. In addition to Langmuir turbulence, ocean surface waves also affect the global climate through wave breaking, wave-current interactions and wave-sea ice interactions etc. Assessing these ocean surface wave effects on global climate is a future direction of my research .

The energy budget in the climate system and climate variability

The poleward transport of the excessive solar energy absorbed at the low latitudes in the atmosphere and the ocean largely determines the regional and global climate variability, which strongly shapes our social and economical activities. For my master's degree, I worked on decomposing the meridional heat transport in the climate system and understanding the climate variability over the past 22,000 years from the energy perspective. See more in my publications [1], [2] and [3].

Multiscale modeling

Conventional parameterizations of vertical turbulent mixing in the ocean surface boundary layer (OSBL) assume a scale separation from the larger scale lateral processes. However, we know that the interactions between the OSBL turbulence and larger scale processes, such as submesoscale fronts and currents, can be important. Simulations that resolve all the important processes across multiple scales are desirable for studying such interactions, but they can be computationally overwhelming. One promissing solution is the multiscale modeling approach, which solves distinct sets of equations for the dynamics at different scales. See more in my publication [12],

GPU accelerated computing

General-purpose graphics processing units (GPUs) have rapidly evolved to become high performance accelerators for modern supercomputers. I have been working on accelerating the large eddy simulations (LES) using GPUs. I have successfully ported a lightweight version of PALM on GPU using OpenACC, a directive-based and performance-portable parallel programming model. Preliminary results with a single GPU show about 10 times speedup versus using a single CPU, though additional speedup is still possible by further optimizing the loops and data locality.
