Langmuir turbulence

Ocean surface waves affect the air-sea fluxes of heat, momentum and gases, thereby the regional and global climate. One important wave-related physical process is Langmuir turbulence, which results from the interaction between the ocean surface waves and the Eulerian currents and turbulence. It strongly affects the vertical mixing in the ocean surface boundary layer (OSBL). I study the physics of Langmuir turbulence using large eddy simulations (LES) of the wave phase averaged equations. The above figure is a snapshot of the vertical velocity in an LES of Langmuir turbulence. Note the streaks of downwelling regions (blue), which are associated with the more well-known Langmuir circulation. See more in my publication [6] and [11].

Turbulence structure & anisotropy

Turbulent flows driven by different forces have distinctive structures and anisotropy. I develop diagnostics to descripe these distinctive features of various turbulent flows. These diagnostics describe the complex structure of turbulent flows in a compact way and provide useful insights into the physics of turbulent flows, including their evolution and interaction with background fluid properties such as the stratification. One example is given by the anisotropic barycentric map and directional map shown above, which help explain the effect of Langmuir turbulence on the entrainment at the base of the ocean mixed layer. See more in my publication [11]. MATLAB tools to generate such plots can be found on Github.

Modeling the OSBL turbulence

Small scale physical processes such as ocean surface boundary layer (OSBL) turbulence are not resolved in climate models, at least not until the end of this century (see Figure 1 of this paper for how this was estimated). However, their effects on the global climate are nontrivial. I develop parameterizations to represent the effects of such small scale physical processes, such as Langmuir turbulence, in climate models. The above figure shows a comparison between 11 OSBL turbulence parameterizations with (the latter 6) and without (the first 5) Langmuir turbulence. Note the signficant discrepancies in both groups. See more in my publication [9]. Many of these parameterizations have been implemented in the Community Ocean Vertical Mixing Project (CVMix) and the General Ocean Turbulence Model (GOTM), and can be easily incorporated in many climate models.
